## 1. Purpose of this document

This note captures the **Strategyn / Ulwick** formulation of JTBD and its operational process, **Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)**. Other writers use “jobs to be done” language differently; this document does not attempt to reconcile every school of thought. It is a practical reference for:

- reframing markets and competition around **jobs** rather than products;
- defining **needs** as **desired outcomes** tied to a **job map**;
- running a **needs-first** innovation sequence before committing to solutions.

---

## 2. Jobs-to-be-Done as a lens

Ulwick describes **Jobs-to-be-Done** first as a **perspective**—a way to observe markets, customers, needs, competitors, and segments. The central shift is methodological:

| Conventional framing | JTBD framing |
|----------------------|--------------|
| Unit of analysis: customer or product | **The core functional job** the customer is trying to get done |
| Market defined by product category or technology | **A group of people** + **the job** they are trying to get done |
| “Customer” often means buyer | **Job executor** (who executes the job with a solution) |
| Needs feel vague or unknowable | **Metrics of success** when executing the job (**desired outcomes**) |
| Competitors = firms with similar products | **Any solution** (including non-obvious substitutes) used to get the job done |
| Segmentation by demographics or psychographics | Segmentation by **how people struggle differently** on the same job |

**Why it matters for innovation:** Products and technologies change; the **job** is treated as comparatively **stable**. If needs are defined as outcomes tied to that job, they too can be **stable over time**, which gives teams durable targets for strategy and product planning instead of chasing moving feature lists.

---

## 3. Jobs-to-be-Done theory (core ideas)

**Theory** here means a set of principles that make marketing and innovation more predictable by centering on the customer’s job-to-be-done.

**Tenets summarized in Ulwick’s writing include:**

1. People buy products and services to get a **job** done.
2. Jobs are **functional**, with **emotional and social** dimensions.
3. A job-to-be-done is **stable over time** (relative to solutions).
4. A job-to-be-done is **solution-agnostic** (not defined by your product).
5. **Success** comes from making the **job**—not the product or the customer persona—the **unit of analysis**.
6. Deep understanding of the job improves **marketing** and makes **innovation** more predictable.
7. Customers want offerings that help them get the job done **better** and/or **more cheaply**.
8. Customers gravitate toward solutions that get the **entire job** done on a **single platform** when possible.
9. **Customer needs**, when tied to the job-to-be-done, make innovation **predictable** (when those needs are defined rigorously—see desired outcomes).

Further tenets and nuance appear in Ulwick’s longer articles and books (e.g. *What Customers Want*, *JOBS TO BE DONE: Theory to Practice*).

## 4. Defining the job-to-be-done

### 4.1 What a job statement is

A **job-to-be-done** is a precise statement of what a **group of people** is trying to **achieve or accomplish** in a given situation: tasks, goals, problems to solve, risks to avoid, or other outcomes they seek.

**Suggested grammatical pattern:**

> **[verb] + [object of the verb] + [optional contextual clarifier]**

**Examples (from the articles):**

- Pass on life lessons to children  
- Repair a torn rotator cuff  
- Prevent a shooter from entering a school  

### 4.2 Defining the job at the right “altitude”

**Too narrow:** You anchor on what your current product does, not the full job the customer is trying to complete. Ulwick’s example: kettle users might say they “boil water,” but the broader job may be **prepare a hot beverage for consumption**. A narrow job definition leaves room for a competitor (e.g. a full beverage system) that **owns the whole job** on one platform.

**Too broad:** The job becomes unactionable or impossible for the organization to serve end-to-end given capabilities, capital, and strategy.

**Practical checks:**

- Do **not** start from “What job did you hire **my product** to do?”—that can truncate the job to a product-shaped slice.
- Ask instead what **underlying objective** or **overall goal** the customer is pursuing when they use your category.
- Ask whether the firm **can and will** address the job **from beginning to end** over time; if not, the definition may be too broad for practical use.

## 5. Job maps (process view of the job)

### 5.1 What a job map is

A **job map** is a **visual decomposition** of the **core functional job** into **discrete process steps**—what the customer is trying to get done, **step by step**.

- It is **not** a customer journey map or purchase journey in the marketing sense.
- It is **not** (in this framework) the sequence of buying, receiving, setting up, and maintaining a product—those belong to **consumption chain** or **purchase** jobs (see §7).

A good job map is **solution-independent**: one map should apply across geographies and competing solutions, describing the **ideal process flow** for getting the job done.

### 5.2 Universal job map structure

Across many jobs, Ulwick and Bettencourt describe **eight fundamental process steps** (jobs may use some or all):

1. Define  
2. Locate  
3. Prepare  
4. Confirm  
5. Execute  
6. Monitor  
7. Modify  
8. Conclude  

The job map exists to:

- set **vision and direction** (e.g. eventually get the **entire** job done on one platform);
- reveal **gaps and opportunities** in how current solutions serve each step;
- guide **discovery of desired outcomes** (metrics of success **per step**).

**Reference:** [The Customer-Centered Innovation Map](https://hbr.org/2008/05/the-customer-centered-innovation-map) (HBR).

## 6. Customer “needs” as desired outcomes

### 6.1 The problem with undefined “needs”

Ulwick argues that much innovation failure traces to teams **not agreeing what a “need” is**, and therefore not being able to **prioritize** unmet needs or **segment** by unmet needs. Raw feature requests and undisciplined interviews do not fix that—they mix solutions with outcomes.

### 6.2 Desired outcome statements

In this framework, **needs** (for innovation purposes) are expressed as **desired outcome statements**: **customer-defined performance metrics** tied to executing the job (often **per job-map step**).

**Properties emphasized in the articles:**

- **Devoid of solutions** (not “give me a microwave”; that’s a solution).
- **Stable over time** (the job’s success metrics persist; solutions change).
- **Measurable** and, for the firm, **controllable** in principle (something you can design toward).
- **Structured** so they can be rated in **quantitative** research (importance, satisfaction).
- **Tied to the job process** (job map), not to your roadmap vocabulary.

**Rough scale (order of magnitude from the articles):**

- **~50–150** desired outcomes for a core functional job.
- **~10–30** per relevant **consumption chain** job.

### 6.3 Syntax for desired outcomes

**Pattern:**

> **Direction of improvement** + **Performance metric** + **Object of control** + **Contextual clarifier**

**Examples (listening to music):**

- Minimize **the time it takes** to get the songs **in the desired order for listening**.  
- Minimize **the likelihood that** the music **sounds distorted at high volume**.

**Why syntax matters:** Inconsistent wording across statements can add noise to survey ratings and distort which outcomes appear “most underserved.”

### 6.4 Discovery methods

Desired outcomes are elicited through **interviews**, **observation**, **ethnography**, **literature review**, and similar—always anchored to the **job** and **job map**, not to feature brainstorming.

## 7. The Jobs-to-be-Done needs framework

The article *A Framework for Customer Needs* organizes **who** has needs and **what kinds** of jobs they are trying to do.

### 7.1 Three customer types

1. **Job executor** — uses the product/service to perform the **core functional job**.
2. **Product lifecycle support team** — people who **install, transport, repair, maintain, upgrade, dispose of**, etc. They execute **consumption chain** jobs.
3. **Buyer** — makes the **purchase decision** (often analyzed with **financial desired outcomes**).

In simple B2C cases, one person may play all three roles. In B2B, roles often split (e.g. surgeon vs. nursing staff vs. hospital purchasing).

### 7.2 Five types of jobs / need categories

**For the job executor:**

1. **Core functional job** — defines the market; the focal job around which value is created.
2. **Related jobs** — additional functional jobs **before, during, or after** the core job; addressing them can increase perceived value.
3. **Emotional jobs** (including **social** aspects) — how the executor wants to **feel** or **be perceived** while doing the core job; used heavily in **value proposition** design.

**Others:**

4. **Consumption chain jobs** — lifecycle tasks around the offering (store, clean, maintain, upgrade, …). The exact set depends on whether the offering is hardware, software, service, or consumable.
5. **Purchase decision job** — how the buyer chooses what to acquire, using **financial** and performance criteria. This is **not** the same as the core functional job; **studying the buying process does not substitute** for understanding the core job.

Together, these categories explain **where** to look for needs and **how** they connect to the stable **core job**.

## 8. Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)

**Outcome-Driven Innovation®** is Strategyn’s **strategy and innovation process** that implements JTBD theory. It combines **qualitative** discovery of outcomes with **quantitative** prioritization and **outcome-based segmentation**.

**Claimed outcomes in the public writing (for context, not independently verified here):** success rates substantially above typical new-product failure rates, when the process is executed with the full inputs (e.g. complete outcome sets, valid quant samples, segmentation).

### 8.1 Diagnosis: why “ideas-first” innovation underperforms

Ulwick’s diagnosis:

- Teams lack a shared definition of **need**.
- They capture only a **subset** of needs.
- They rarely **quantify** which needs are unmet and by how much.
- They miss **segments** whose unmet outcomes **differ**.
- Without a clear target, **idea generation** is a **guess**; “fail fast” does not fix aiming at the wrong outcome.

**Contrast: needs-first.** Define the full set of customer needs as outcomes, **quantify** under- and overservice, **segment** by unmet outcomes, **then** ideate and evaluate concepts **against the same metrics**—ideally **before** heavy development spend.

### 8.2 The ODI process (six steps)

The long-form ODI article presents **six steps**. Strategyn’s public FAQ aligns with the following sequence:

1. **Define the market around the job-to-be-done**  
   Market = **people + core functional job**. Build a **job map**.

2. **Uncover the customer’s desired outcomes**  
   Capture the **complete** set of outcome statements for the core job (and relevant consumption chain jobs as needed), using the **needs framework**.

3. **Quantify which outcomes are over- or underserved**  
   Survey a statistically meaningful sample; rate outcomes for **importance** and **satisfaction**. Apply an **opportunity algorithm** (or similar) to rank outcomes (underserved = important but unsatisfied; overserved = unimportant but highly satisfied—room to **simplify** or **reduce cost**).

4. **Discover hidden segments of opportunity**  
   **Outcome-based segmentation**: groups with **distinct bundles of unmet outcomes** (not discoverable by demographics alone).

5. **Align existing products with market opportunities**  
   Positioning, messaging, portfolio moves, competitive response—using **which outcomes** each segment cares about and which current solutions address poorly.

6. **Conceptualize new products and services**  
   Ideation is **targeted** at prioritized underserved outcomes for chosen segments; concepts are checked against the **same outcome metrics** early (concept testing in JTBD terms = **better at helping customers get the job done** than alternatives).

**Note:** Some Strategyn web pages summarize a **five-step** front-end for communication; the **six-step** FAQ explicitly splits **align existing offerings** and **conceptualize new** offerings. The substance is one continuous pipeline from market definition through strategy and concepts.

### 8.3 Quantification details (typical in ODI descriptions)

- Sample sizes in published materials often cited on the order of **hundreds to a few thousand** respondents for quant phases (exact targets depend on market and statistics).
- **Competitive insight:** different solutions can be evaluated by how well users rate satisfaction on **each outcome**—a job-based competitive view, not only feature checklists.

### 8.4 Cordis case (historical anchor)

The 1990s **Cordis** angioplasty balloon work is often cited: job framed as restoring blood flow in a blocked artery; **desired outcomes** surfaced and prioritized; multiple successful launches. See HBR’s **Turn Customer Input into Innovation** for the published case narrative.

## 9. Where JTBD applies inside an organization

Ulwick lists four **distinct** application areas (each may need different tools):

1. **Market selection** — which **jobs / markets** to enter; attractiveness (e.g. how many executors, frequency, degree of underserved outcomes). Market evaluation templates exist in Strategyn’s public materials.
2. **Product planning / innovation** — the core **ODI** loop: outcomes, quantification, segmentation, strategy, concepts.
3. **Product development** — often focuses on **consumption chain** outcomes (learn, interface, maintain, etc.) and **job/outcome stories** for developers and UX; works best when **upstream** ODI has already clarified the core job and prioritized outcomes (avoid merging **innovation discovery** and **build cycles** into one undifferentiated scramble).
4. **Buying process** — improving the **purchase job** for customers (research, evaluation, transaction). **Separate** from the core functional job; insights here improve **go-to-market and experience**, not substitute for core-job understanding.

## 10. Important distinctions (summary)

| Topic | Correct in this framework |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Job map vs. journey | Job map = **what** they’re trying to accomplish in process steps; **not** the buying or onboarding journey. |
| Core job vs. purchase | **Purchase** is its own job with **financial** outcomes; do not conflate with the **core functional** job. |
| Customer vs. executor | “Customer” in innovation often means **job executor**; **buyer** may be a different person with different metrics. |
| Need vs. solution | **Microwave** is a solution; **needs** are outcomes like speed, even cooking, cleanup—expressed without naming the product. |
| Idea timing | **Ideas are outputs** of a disciplined needs process, not the arbitrary **starting point** if predictability is the goal. |
